# Station Four

The first train arrives at 5:47 AM.

Thomas has known this for forty-three years. Not from the timetable—though that confirms it—but from the way the rails hum in the dark, a low vibration that travels up through the platform concrete, through the worn soles of his boots, into his bones. The 5:47 doesn't stop here anymore. Hasn't for six years. But it passes, and Thomas feels it, and the day begins.

He unlocks the station house at 5:30, same as always.

The key turns in the lock with a familiar resistance—needs oil, has needed oil for months—and the door swings inward on hinges that creak in E-flat. Inside, the waiting room smells of old wood and dust and something else, something harder to name. Memory, perhaps. Or time itself, settling into corners.

Thomas doesn't turn on the main lights yet. Just the small lamp on the ticket desk, which casts a warm circle that doesn't quite reach the walls. He likes this hour, this in-between time when the station belongs only to him and the ghosts of a hundred thousand journeys.

He fills the kettle. Sets it on the hot plate. Pulls his thermos from the shelf.

The morning routine is a kind of prayer.

---

The station is called Pennant Halt, though most maps have forgotten it. Four platforms, two of them crumbling back into the earth. A footbridge with peeling green paint. A waiting room with wooden benches worn smooth by decades of restless hands.

Once, this was a junction. Trains split here: north to Aberystwyth, west to Carmarthen, south down the valley to the coast. Once, there were families with suitcases, businessmen with briefcases, lovers saying goodbye on platforms 2 and 3.

Now there is Thomas, and two trains a day, and a timetable that reads like an elegy.

---

The kettle boils.

Thomas pours water over tea leaves—loose, not bagged, a small vanity—and watches the color bloom. Steam rises, catches the lamplight, becomes briefly, beautifully visible before vanishing into the dark above.

He thinks about visibility. About the things we see and the things we don't.

The 6:15 is the first train that stops. A two-car diesel unit, older than it should be, rattling down from Machynlleth. It brings the morning paper, a handful of commuters, and—three times a week—a woman with silver hair who never speaks but always nods.

Thomas is ready.

---

Platform 1 is swept clean. The benches are checked. The timetable board—mechanical, clicking over with satisfying precision—shows the correct times. Everything in order.

This is what matters: that when someone arrives, even if it's just one person, even if it's just for a moment, the station is ready.

Thomas learned this from Mr. Davies, who taught him the job in 1981. "A railway station," Mr. Davies had said, "is a threshold. People come here between one place and another, one life and another. Your job isn't to hurry them through. It's to hold the space. To keep the threshold clean."

Thomas has kept it clean.

---

The 6:15 arrives on time.

Three passengers disembark: two young men in hi-vis jackets, heading to the wind farm construction site five miles up the valley, and Mrs. Anwen Hughes, who has taken this train every Wednesday for as long as Thomas can remember.

"Bore da, Thomas," she says, her voice soft as worn velvet.

"Bore da, Mrs. Hughes. Lovely morning."

"Aye. The light on the hills is beautiful today."

They have this conversation, or ones like it, every week. Thomas could not tell you when it began. It has always been.

Mrs. Hughes walks slowly across the footbridge, her shopping bag in one hand, her other trailing along the railing. Thomas watches to make sure she reaches the other side safely. She always does. He always watches.

---

The morning unfolds in its patterns.

7:23: Schoolchildren. Loud, chaotic, full of life that makes the station feel briefly young again.

8:15: The southbound service. Usually empty. Sometimes a walker, heading for the coastal path.

9:30: Nothing. The gap in the timetable. Thomas drinks more tea, reads yesterday's paper (today's hasn't arrived yet), listens to the wind in the eaves.

This is when he sometimes walks the platforms, checking things that don't need checking. The signal box—defunct, locked—stands at the south end of platform 4. Thomas has a key but hasn't used it in years. Inside, the levers are still in place, brass handles worn smooth. The logbook still sits on the desk, the last entry dated June 3rd, 1997. "All clear. Weather fine. J. Morris."

J. Morris retired three days later. Thomas sometimes wonders if he knew it would be his last entry. If he chose those words deliberately. "All clear."

---

The postwoman arrives at 10:00.

Not a train. Not a passenger. But part of the pattern nonetheless.

"Morning, Thomas. Quiet one?"

"Same as always, Rhian."

She hands him the bundle: mostly bills for the railway company, occasionally something personal. Today, there's a catalog. Thomas doesn't remember ordering it, but he thanks her anyway.

They talk for a few minutes about nothing important—the weather, the roadworks in town, her grandson's new job. Then she's gone, her van rattling back up the lane, and Thomas is alone again.

He doesn't mind being alone.

---

Lunchtime, he makes a sandwich in the back room—cheese and tomato, same as always—and eats it on platform 2, watching the valley.

The hills rise on three sides, patchworked with fields and forests. In summer, they're a dozen shades of green. In autumn, gold and rust and copper. Winter, they turn gray and stern.

Today, early April, they're uncertain. Green trying to break through winter's brown. Life returning, but tentatively.

A buzzard circles overhead, riding thermals Thomas can't see. He watches it for a long time. It doesn't land. Just circles, searching, patient.

Thomas understands the searching.

---

The 12:45 brings a surprise: a family.

Mother, father, two small children. The parents are arguing quietly, the children oblivious, focused on a shared tablet. They have suitcases, the kind with wheels that clatter badly over the platform's uneven surface.

The father approaches the ticket window.

"Excuse me, we need tickets to Cardiff."

Thomas nods, reaches for the booking terminal. It's older than the children. Sometimes it works.

"When do you need to travel?"

"Now. Next train?"

Thomas checks the timetable. "Next southbound is 2:30. Gets into Cardiff at 5:15, with a change at Bridgend."

The father's face tightens. "Nothing sooner?"

"No, sir. I'm afraid not."

The mother sits heavily on a bench, pulling the children close. One of them—a girl, maybe six—looks up at Thomas with enormous brown eyes.

"Is this a real train station?" she asks.

"It is," Thomas says.

"It's very small."

"It is that too."

The father sighs, pulls out his wallet. Thomas issues the tickets, the printer grinding through the cardstock with its usual reluctance. The family settles in to wait.

Thomas makes them tea without asking. Brings it in proper cups, with a small jug of milk and bowl of sugar. The mother looks up, surprised.

"Oh, you didn't have to—"

"It's no trouble."

She accepts the tea, wraps both hands around the cup like it's anchoring her to the platform. Thomas returns to his desk, gives them space.

Through the window, he watches them. The father pacing. The mother staring at nothing. The children slowly realizing that boredom is a real and present danger.

Then the girl—the one who asked about the station—stands up and walks to the edge of platform 2. She's looking at something. Thomas can't see what.

He steps outside.

"See something interesting?"

She points. "That bird. The one with the red."

A robin, perched on the old signal box. Singing its territorial song.

"That's a robin," Thomas says. "They're very brave. Not afraid of people."

"Why?"

"I'm not sure. Maybe they know we won't hurt them."

The girl considers this. "I wouldn't hurt it."

"I believe you."

They stand together, watching the robin. It finishes its song, flicks its tail, flies to a fence post closer to them.

"It's saying hello," the girl whispers.

Thomas smiles. "I think you're right."

---

The 2:30 arrives, departs. The family boards. The girl waves to Thomas from the window as the train pulls away.

Then: silence again.

Platform 2 feels emptier than it did before. The robin is gone. Thomas picks up the teacups, rinses them in the back room sink, sets them to dry.

He's learned not to get attached to the passengers. They come, they go. That's what passengers do. But sometimes—not often, but sometimes—one leaves a mark. A small impression on the day, like a footprint in fresh snow.

The girl with the brown eyes. The robin.

Thomas adds it to the accumulation of small moments that make up a life.

---

The afternoon is quiet.

4:15: The northbound service. Two passengers board, neither familiar.

5:30: Nothing scheduled. Thomas sweeps platform 1 anyway, redistributes the gravel that's migrated from the track bed.

6:00: The last train south for the day. Sometimes it carries workers heading home from the wind farm. Today, it's empty when it arrives and empty when it leaves.

The driver leans out briefly. "Quiet one, Thomas?"

"Same as always."

The driver grins. "See you tomorrow."

"God willing."

It's what they always say. A small ritual. Thomas isn't particularly religious, but he likes the rhythm of it. The acknowledgment that nothing is guaranteed, that every tomorrow is a small gift.

The train pulls away, its diesel engine loud in the valley. Then quiet again.

---

Thomas locks the station house at 6:30.

Before he does, he checks everything one last time. Lights off. Kettle unplugged. Windows secure. The lamp on the ticket desk off. The door locked. Key turned, satisfying resistance, done.

He walks to his car—a faded blue Volvo, older than it should be—and sits for a moment before starting the engine.

From here, he can see all four platforms. The footbridge. The empty space where platform 3 used to have a shelter before the storm in '09 took it. The signal box, silhouetted against the evening sky.

Forty-three years.

Next week will be his last.

---

Thomas doesn't sleep well.

He lives alone, in a cottage three miles from the station. There's a photo on the mantelpiece—him and Susan on their wedding day, 1979. She died in 2003. Cancer. Quick and cruel.

No children. That was always going to be complicated, and then it was too late, and then Susan was gone.

The cottage is clean but not tidy. Books everywhere, mostly about railways. A kettle on the stove. A chair by the window where Thomas sits and watches the hills.

Tonight, he doesn't feel like reading. He makes tea, sits in the chair, looks out at the dark.

Retirement. The word feels wrong in his mouth.

The railway company has been generous, he supposes. A pension. A small ceremony if he wants one. (He doesn't.) A gold-plated pocket watch, because apparently that's traditional.

"You've earned it," the regional manager had said. "Forty-three years. My God, Thomas. That's a lifetime."

It is. It has been.

But what comes after?

---

The days blur together.

Monday: Same patterns. 5:47 hum. 6:15 arrival. Mrs. Hughes on Wednesday's early run—no, it's Monday, she's not here. The schoolchildren. The gap at 9:30. Rhian with the post.

Tuesday: A walker asks about the coastal path. Thomas gives him directions, watches him set off across the footbridge with a rucksack and hiking poles. The man looks back once, waves. Thomas waves back.

Wednesday: Mrs. Hughes. "Bore da, Thomas." "Bore da, Mrs. Hughes." The light on the hills. The ritual unchanged.

Thursday: Rain. Heavy, persistent. The platforms slick and dark. Only four passengers all day. Thomas drinks extra tea, reads the paper cover to cover, including the classifieds.

Friday: A near miss.

The 4:15 northbound arrives, and as it's pulling away, a young man runs onto the platform, shouting. He's missed it. By seconds.

He stands there, shoulders slumped, staring at the receding train.

Thomas opens the ticket window. "Next one's at 6:00."

The young man turns. He looks exhausted. "Thanks. Yeah. I'll wait."

"There's tea if you want it."

"That'd be great. Thank you."

Thomas makes the tea, brings it out. The young man is sitting on a bench, head in his hands.

"Rough day?" Thomas asks.

The young man laughs, hollow. "You could say that. Lost my job. In Aberystwyth. Drove down to tell my parents. Now I'm going back to pack my stuff."

Thomas nods. Doesn't offer platitudes. Sometimes people just need to be heard.

"This place is really quiet," the young man says after a while.

"It is."

"Do you like it? Working here?"

Thomas considers the question. "Yes. I think I do. Or I did. I'm retiring next week."

"Oh. Congratulations?"

"Thank you."

They sit in companionable silence. The rain eases. A patch of sunlight breaks through the clouds, illuminates the valley for a few brief, golden moments.

"That's beautiful," the young man says quietly.

"It is."

The 6:00 arrives. The young man boards, carrying his tea with him. Through the window, he mouths "thank you." Thomas nods.

Another small moment. Another footprint.

---

Saturday doesn't feel different, but it is.

The last Saturday. After this, one more week Monday through Friday, and then done.

The morning is cold but clear. The 5:47 hum. The kettle. The tea. Platform 1 swept. Timetable checked.

And then, at 7:00, something unusual: a train that isn't scheduled.

It appears on the southbound line, moving slowly. Not a passenger service. A maintenance train, or perhaps something being repositioned. It doesn't stop, but it slows as it passes, and Thomas sees the driver's hand rise in a wave.

He waves back.

The train disappears around the bend, and then it's gone, and Thomas Is left with an unexpected feeling in his chest. Not sadness, exactly. Not quite joy either. Something older, deeper. Recognition, perhaps. A sense of being seen by someone who understands what it means to keep the rails warm.

---

Sunday, Thomas doesn't go to the station.

Sundays have been his day off for forty-three years. He usually does laundry, grocery shopping, small maintenance around the cottage. Today, he doesn't feel like doing any of it.

Instead, he drives to the coast.

It's an hour each way, but the road is quiet and the day is fine. He parks at a beach he's been to exactly three times: once with Susan, once alone after she died, and now.

The sea is gray and restless. The beach is empty except for a dog walker in the distance. Thomas sits on a bench overlooking the water and tries to imagine what comes next.

Watching television. Gardening, maybe. The library in town has volunteering opportunities. He could travel, theoretically. There's money saved.

But none of it feels real. None of it feels like *him*.

He is Station Four. He is the 5:47 hum and the lamp on the ticket desk and the sweep of platform 1. Without it, what is he?

The question doesn't have an answer. Not yet. Maybe not ever.

Thomas drives home as the sun sets. The cottage is cold. He lights the fire, makes dinner, eats it standing at the kitchen counter. Goes to bed early.

Tomorrow is Monday. Four days left.

---

Monday morning, Thomas arrives at 5:30 to find someone waiting on platform 2.

This has never happened before.

It's an old man—older than Thomas, which is saying something—sitting on a bench with a small suitcase beside him. He's wearing a wool coat and a flat cap, and when he sees Thomas, he stands.

"Morning. I'm early. I'm sorry#."

"No need to apologize," Thomas says, unlocking the station house. "First train's at 6:15, though. You've got a bit of a wait."

"I know. I wanted to be sure."

Thomas nods. Makes tea. Brings a cup to the old man, who accepts it with trembling hands.

"Thank you. You're very kind."

They don't talk beyond that. The old man sits, drinking his tea, staring down the line as if willing the train to appear early. Thomas tends to the morning routine, but his mind is on the man. Something about him is familiar.

And then he realizes.

"Excuse me," Thomas says, stepping onto platform 2. "This is going to sound odd, but... did you used to work here?"

The old man looks up, surprised. Then he smiles.

"Yes. Long time ago. I was a porter, 1955 to '63. Before they closed platform 4."

"J. Morris?"

The smile widens. "Good Lord. You know my name?"

Thomas fetches the signal box key.

---

They stand in the signal box together, dust motes dancing in the morning light.

J. Morris—John, he insists—runs his hand along the levers, and Thomas sees tears in his eyes.

"I thought about this place often," John says quietly. "After I left. After everything changed. I thought about it, but I never came back. Didn't think I could stand it."

"What made you come today?"

John is silent for a long moment. Then: "My wife died last month. Fifty-eight years married. And I woke up this morning thinking, what am I still here for? And I thought of this place. I thought... maybe I needed to say goodbye properly."

Thomas understands.

They stand together in the signal box, two old men looking at machinery that no longer serves its purpose, and Thomas feels the accumulation of all the years pressing down. Not just his years. John's too. And everyone who ever passed through this place, everyone who waited on these platforms, everyone who kept the threshold clean.

"I'm retiring next Friday," Thomas says.

John nods. "End of an era."

"Yes."

"Will they close it?"

"I don't know. Probably not right away. But eventually. Yes."

The 6:15 arrives on time. John boards, his small suitcase in hand. Through the window, he and Thomas look at each other one last time.

The train pulls away.

Thomas locks the signal box. Returns the key to its hook.

---

The week passes.

Tuesday: Quiet. Wednesday: Mrs. Hughes. Thursday: Rain again. Friday: The last Friday.

And then it's Monday morning, and Thomas is unlocking the station house for the last time.

The key turns. The door swings. The lamp goes on.

The kettle boils.

The tea steeps.

Outside, the 5:47 hums through the rails, doesn't stop, never stopped, but is felt nonetheless.

Thomas carries his tea to platform 2 and watches the valley wake up.

He thinks about J. Morris, about the girl with the brown eyes, about Mrs. Hughes, about the young man who lost his job. About Susan, who never liked the station but understood why he did. About forty-three years of small moments, small kindnesses, small rituals that add up to a life.

The 6:15 will arrive soon.

And then the 8:15.

And then the gap at 9:30, which Thomas will spend reading the paper and drinking tea and keeping the threshold clean one last time.

The patterns hold.

Until they don't.

But for now—for this morning, this moment—Thomas is exactly where he's supposed to be.

The station is ready.

The threshold is clean.

And somewhere in the valley, a robin sings.
