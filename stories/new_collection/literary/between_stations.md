# Between Stations

The train from Kraków to Berlin stops at eight stations, but Lin exists between them.

She's been traveling for six years now—not on this train specifically, but on trains like it. Buses. Ferries. Sometimes planes when she has the money. Always moving, always between one place and another, never quite arriving.

Her mother calls it running away. Lin calls it existing in translation.

---

Lin was born in Taipei and raised in Toronto. At least, that's the simple version. The complicated version involves her father's tech job bringing the family to Canada when she was seven, her parents' divorce when she was twelve, shuttling between her mother's apartment in Chinatown and her father's condo in the financial district. Two homes, two languages, two sets of expectations.

She never felt whole in either place.

At nineteen, she left for university in Montreal. At twenty-two, she moved to London for graduate school. At twenty-four, she dropped out and started moving.

Bangkok. Prague. Istanbul. Lisbon. Cities where she could be foreign without explanation, where her in-between-ness felt like freedom rather than failure.

Now she's thirty, on this train, watching Poland turn into Germany through a fingerprint-smudged window.

---

The woman across from Lin is reading in German, her lips moving slightly with the words. She's maybe sixty, with practical short hair and the weathered elegance of someone who's lived through things worth surviving.

"Entschuldigung," the woman says, then switches to accented English. "You are American?"

"Canadian," Lin says, which is true and not true.

"Ah. Beautiful country. I visited Vancouver once." The woman extends her hand. "Petra."

"Lin."

They shake, and Petra returns to her book. But ten minutes later, at the snack cart, Petra buys two coffees and offers one to Lin.

"For the journey," she says.

Lin has learned that trains make people generous like this. Something about shared motion creates temporary kinship.

---

Over coffee in paper cups, Petra tells her story.

She's traveling to Berlin to see her grandson for the first time. Her daughter married a Kenyan man and moved to Nairobi five years ago. The grandson, Leo, was born three months ago, but visiting hadn't been possible until now—Petra's husband was ill, then died, and grief takes its own timeline.

"I don't know what to bring a baby in Africa," Petra says. "Everything I buy seems wrong. Too European. Too assuming."

"I think babies just want to be held," Lin offers.

Petra smiles sadly. "Yes. But I don't know if I remember how."

Lin thinks about distance—not just geographic, but emotional. The miles we put between ourselves and the people we love. Sometimes deliberately, sometimes accidentally, sometimes because staying still feels impossible.

"You'll remember," Lin says, hoping it's true.

---

At the Polish-German border, police board the train to check passports.

Lin's Canadian passport opens easily for her—an inheritance of colonial history and diplomatic relationships she didn't choose but benefits from. She watches as other passengers are questioned more thoroughly: the young men with Syrian passports, the family speaking a language Lin doesn't recognize, the elderly woman whose documents seem insufficient.

Petra watches too, her face carefully neutral, and Lin wonders what memories she's carrying from a Germany that once divided itself with walls and checkpoints.

The police leave. The train continues.

Lin feels the familiar weight of her privilege—the invisible passport that lets her treat borders as adventures rather than barriers.

---

Petra asks where Lin is going.

"Berlin," Lin says. "Maybe. I haven't decided."

"You haven't decided if you're going to Berlin? But you're on the train to Berlin."

"I'm on the train, yes. But I might get off early. Or late. I don't know."

Petra considers this. "Are you running from something or toward something?"

It's the question Lin has been avoiding for six years.

"Neither," she says. "Both. I don't know."

"That's honest, at least."

---

Lin started traveling because staying still felt like suffocating.

After dropping out of graduate school in London, she'd moved back to Toronto and tried to rebuild. Got a job at a marketing agency. Dated a man named David who wanted to buy a condo and talk about retirement savings. Went to Sunday dinners at her mother's apartment where the questions about marriage and career sat heavy between the soup dumplings.

She lasted eight months before booking a one-way ticket to Thailand.

Her mother called it selfish. Her father called it irresponsible. David called it a quarter-life crisis and suggested therapy.

Lin called it necessary.

She found work teaching English online, hours flexible enough to accommodate time zones and transience. She lived cheaply, moved frequently, collected experience like other people collect possessions.

She told herself she was finding herself. Learning who she was outside of everyone's expectations.

But six years in, she's starting to wonder if you can find yourself when you never stop moving long enough to look.

---

The train passes through small German towns, their stations decorated with flower boxes and impeccable punctuality. Each platform looks the same but different—the particular sameness of European efficiency.

Petra has fallen asleep, her book open on her lap.

Lin opens her laptop and answers emails for her students in South Korea and Japan. She's teaching business English to professionals who need to sound confident in meetings, who need to smooth the edges of their accents, who need to translate themselves for success.

Lin is good at translation. She's been doing it her whole life—translating between her mother's Mandarin and Toronto's English, between her father's ambitions and her own uncertainties, between who she's supposed to be and who she might actually be.

A message appears from her mother: "When are you coming home?"

Lin closes the laptop without answering.

---

Home. The word sits uncomfortably in Lin's mouth.

Is it Toronto, where she grew up but never quite belonged? Is it Taipei, where her grandmother still lives in an apartment Lin has visited three times? Is it the hostel in Prague where she stayed for four months and knew everyone's name? Is it this train, where she exists in perfect suspension between destinations?

Maybe home isn't a place. Maybe it's just the feeling of not having to explain yourself.

---

At Frankfurt, the train stops for twenty minutes.

Petra wakes, disoriented, and Lin helps her gather her things. They buy sandwiches from the platform vendor—overpriced, under-flavored, exactly what train travelers deserve.

"I'm nervous," Petra admits, unwrapping her sandwich. "To meet Leo. What if he cries when I hold him? What if I say the wrong things to my daughter? We haven't spoken much since her father died. I said things... in grief, you say things."

Lin recognizes herself in this—the fear of connection, the way distance can feel safer than presence, the things unsaid that pile up like unopened mail.

"Maybe you just start with 'I'm sorry,'" Lin suggests. "And see what happens next."

Petra nods, blinks back tears, takes a bite of bad sandwich.

---

Back on the train, Petra asks about Lin's family.

Lin gives the edited version: parents divorced, lives in different cities, complicated relationship. She doesn't mention the arguments about her "wasted potential," the pressure to settle down, the way her mother cries on the phone when Lin mentions moving to another country.

"You love them," Petra observes. It's not a question.

"Yes. distanc sometimes."

"Love requires presence," Petra says gently. "Not always physical, but... present. You can't love someone you're always leaving."

The words land like stones in water, sending ripples Lin doesn't want to examine.

---

At Hanover, two young backpackers board the train—Australian, loud, excited about everything. They plop into nearby seats and immediately start making friends with everyone in the car.

"Where you from?" one asks Lin.

"Canada. Taiwan. Nowhere. Everywhere," Lin says, and they laugh like it's a joke.

"We're from Brisbane," the other says. "Doing Europe for three months before we have to get real jobs. This is our last big adventure."

They say "last" like it's a period at the end of a sentence. Like they know when adventures are supposed to end and real life begins.

Lin envies their certainty.

---

The Australians pull out a map—an actual paper map, novelty in the age of GPS—and trace their route across Europe. They've planned everything meticulously: which museums, which hostels, which Instagram photos.

"What about you?" they ask Lin. "What's your route?"

Lin thinks about the question. Her route for the past six years has been anywhere-but-here. Toward whatever seemed interesting. Away from whatever felt too permanent.

"I'm figuring it out," she says.

They nod enthusiastically, as if this is profound rather than avoidant.

---

Petra catches Lin's eye and smiles knowingly.

They're approaching Berlin now, the outskirts turning into suburbs turning into city. The train picks up speed, eager to arrive, indifferent to Lin's desire to stay in transit forever.

"What will you do in Berlin?" Petra asks.

"I don't know. Find a hostel. Maybe stay a week. Maybe longer."

"And then?"

"Somewhere else. Copenhagen, maybe. Or south—Budapest, Sofia."

"You have money for all this traveling?"

"I work online. Teach English. Live cheaply. It works."

"But does it fill you?"

The question catches Lin off guard. She's spent six years convincing everyone—herself included—that this life is enough. That freedom equals fulfillment. That not belonging anywhere means you can belong everywhere.

But sitting here, with Berlin approaching and Petra waiting for an answer, Lin feels the emptiness she's been outrunning.

"Sometimes," she says honestly.

---

The train pulls into Berlin Hauptbahnhof at 16:47, exactly on time.

Passengers gather their luggage, their jackets, their children. The Australians bounce with excitement. Petra moves slowly, carefully, a woman preparing herself for a reunion she both wants and fears.

Lin helps Petra with her bag, walks with her to the platform.

"Thank you for the conversation," Petra says. "And for listening."

"Thank you for the coffee."

They stand in the station's chaos—announcements in German and English, trains departing and arriving, people meeting and parting.

"Can I tell you something?" Petra says. "From an old woman who has run away and come back and run away again?"

Lin nods.

"Running is sometimes necessary. But at some point, you have to stop long enough to let life catch up to you. Otherwise you're just..." She gestures at the trains around them. "Always in transit. Never arriving."

She hugs Lin—quick, firm, grandmotherly—and disappears into the crowd.

---

Lin finds a hostel near Alexanderplatz. The room is institutional and anonymous, exactly like hostels everywhere. She's shared rooms like this in forty-seven cities across six continents.

She unpacks, which means transferring clothes from backpack to a locker. She's perfected the art of living compact: seven outfits, toiletries, laptop, passport, always ready to leave.

That night, she joins other travelers in the hostel's common room. There's the usual mix: gap-year students, digital nomads, middle-aged divorce escapees, young people figuring things out, old people starting over.

Everyone has a story about where they've been and where they're going.

No one talks about why they left or what they're avoiding.

---

Lin spends a week in Berlin doing tourist things: the Brandenburg Gate, the East Side Gallery, Museum Island. She takes photos for her Instagram—carefully cupped to suggest adventure and spontaneity, though really they're calculated as any advertisement.

She meets up with other travelers for beers and broken conversations about hostels and trains and travel hacks. They trade recommendations and warning stories, perform the ritual of transient friendship.

On her eighth night, Lin sits alone in a park near the hostel and opens her laptop.

Her mother has sent three more messages, each one a mixture of concern and accusation. Her father has sent money with a note: "In case you need it." Her friend from graduate school—her only friend who still keeps in touch—has sent photos of her wedding, to which Lin was invited but didn't attend.

Lin scrolls through the photos: the church, the reception, the happiness that comes from committing to place and person and future.

She feels simultaneously above it all and desperately left out.

---

That night, Lin has a video call with one of her students—ayoung woman in Tokyo preparing for a job interview at an international company.

"I'm nervous about being myself," the student says in careful English. "They want someone confident and... Western. But that's not me."

"You can be yourself and confident," Lin says. "They're not opposites."

"But yourself is not enough sometimes. You have to translate yourself to fit."

Lin hears herself in these words. She's been translating herself for so long she's not sure what the original language was.

"What if," Lin says slowly, "being yourself is enough? What if the translation is what gets lost?"

The student looks confused. Lin isn't sure she understands her own question.

---

In her second week in Berlin, Lin takes a class in German at a community center.

She's not planning to stay—she never plans to stay—but learning basics feels responsible. "Guten Tag. Danke. Entschuldigung. Wo ist der Bahnhof?"

The class is full of migrants and refugees, people learning German because they have to, not because they want to. Their language books are worn, their faces serious. This isn't a gap year adventure. This is survival.

Lin feels like an impostor. She's choosing to be unattached. They're trying desperately to attach.

After class, a Syrian woman named Amal approaches Lin.

"You are traveling for fun?" she asks.

"I guess so," Lin says.

"Must be nice. To choose when to stop."

The words aren't cruel, just factual. But they land like an indictment.

---

Lin starts going to the German class three times a week.

She befriends Amal, who has a doctorate in engineering from Damascus and is working at a grocery store while her credentials are verified—a process that's been ongoing for two years.

"In Syria, I designed water systems," Amal says over coffee after class. "Here, I stock shelves. But I'm grateful. My family is safe. That's what matters."

Lin asks why Berlin.

"Germany accepted us. And my sister was already here. Family pulls, you know?"

Lin doesn't know, actually. She's spent six years pulling away from family, not toward it.

---

Amal invites Lin to dinner with her sister's family.

The apartment is small, crowded, full of the particular noise of children and adults navigating life together. Amal's sister, Reem, is pregnant with her third child. Her husband, Karim, works two jobs and is learning German through night classes.

They feed Lin mezze and stories, insisting she eat more even when she's full. The children show her their schoolwork, their drawings, their careful German homework.

"Do you have family here?" Reem asks.

"No. I'm traveling."

"For how long?"

"I don't know. Six years so far."

Reem looks confused. "You've been traveling six years? Where is your home?"

"I don't really have one."

"Everyone has a home," Reem says, certain in the way of people who've had to fight for theirs.

Later, helping wash dishes, Amal says quietly, "She doesn't understand choosing homelessness. For us, home is everything we lost. We're trying to build it again."

Lin feels the weight of her privilege again—that she can treat roots as optional.

---

That night, lying in her hostel bunk, Lin thinks about the word "home."

For Amal and her family, it's something to build toward. For Petra, it was something to return to. For her mother, it's Toronto, immovable and certain. For her father, it's wherever success leads.

For Lin, it's been the absence of commitment. The freedom of nowhere.

But maybe freedom and emptiness aren't so different.

---

In her third week, Lin gets an offer for a job.

One of her regular students—a tech company executive in Seoul—asks if she'd consider moving to Korea to work in-house teaching English to their international team. Good salary, visa sponsorship, apartment provided.

It's the kind of opportunity she used to dream about. Stability with just enough foreignness to feel like adventure.

She tells them she'll think about it.

But thinking about it means imagining staying somewhere longer than a month. Means unpacking more than seven outfits. Means being present enough that people learn her routines, her coffee order, her last name.

The thought makes her want to book a train to anywhere else.

---

Lin calls her father. It's the first time in four months.

"I got a job offer," she says. "In Seoul."

"That's wonderful!" He sounds genuinely excited. "Are you going to take it?"

"I don't know."

"What's stopping you?"

Lin doesn't know how to explain that commitment feels like burial. That she's spent six years convinced that stopping means failing, that arrival means death of possibility.

"I'm just not sure I'm ready to settle down," she says.

Her father is quiet for a long time.

"Lin," he finally says, "I moved from Taipei to Toronto when you were seven. Do you know why?"

"Your job."

"Yes, but do you know why I took that job? I could have found work in Taipei. Your mother wanted to stay. But I thought... I thought success meant leaving. Moving up and out. I thought roots were for people without ambition."

Lin has never heard him talk like this.

"I spent thirty years building a career in Canada. Made good money. Good reputation. But you know what I lost? Your mother. Connection to the place I was from. The language, the food, the parts of myself that didn't fit in Toronto boardrooms."

"Are you saying I should take the job?"

"I'm saying that running away from roots doesn't make you free. It just makes you alone."

After they hang up, Lin sits with his words. She's always thought of her father as the model of successful immigration—adaptable, ambitious, unsentiment. She never considered what he might have lost in the translation.

---

The next German class, Amal brings news: her credentials have finally been approved. She's got an engineering job starting next month.

The class celebrates with grocery-store cake and terrible coffee.

"Will you stay in Berlin?" Lin asks.

"Of course. My life is here now. My sister, my community, my job. You plant roots, they grow."

"What if the roots feel like chains?"

Amal gives her a sharp look. "Roots are why trees don't fall over. You think it's freedom to blow around like a leaf? Maybe for a while. But eventually, you just dry up."

---

Lin decides to extend her hostel stay. Not forever—she can't think in terms of forever—but another month. Maybe two.

She takes the job in Seoul. Tells them she can start in sixty days.

Sixty days to practice being still, being present, being somewhere long enough to be known.

She's terrified.

---

In her fourth week, Lin does something she hasn't done in six years: she buys plants.

Two small succulents from a market near the hostel. Traveling with plants is absurd and impractical and exactly the kind of commitment she's been avoiding.

But she buys them anyway. Names them Margaret and Petra, after women on trains who told her true things.

---

She calls her mother.

"I got a job," Lin says. "In Seoul. I'm taking it."

"For how long?"

"A year contract. Maybe more. I don't know."

Her mother is quiet, and Lin can hear the calculation: a year is longer than Lin has stayed anywhere in six years, but it's still not Toronto, still not close, still not settled in the way her mother imagined.

"Are you happy?" her mother finally asks.

It's not the question Lin expected.

"I don't know. But I'm trying to be."

"That's something, I suppose."

They talk for an hour—the longest conversation they've had since Lin left London. Her mother tells her about the restaurant that closed, the neighbor who died, the way Toronto is changing. Lin tells her about Berlin, about Petra and Amal, about the plants she bought called Margaret and Petra.

"You named plants?" Her mother sounds amused. "That's very permanent of you."

"Baby steps."

Before they hang up, her mother says, "You know, bǎobǎo, home isn't a trap. It's just... where people know you. Really know you. That's scary, but it's also..."

"What?"

"Everything."

---

Lin spends her last two weeks in Berlin actually experiencing Berlin.

Not as a tourist passing through, but as someone temporarily living there. She finds a favorite café and becomes a regular. She walks the same routes enough to recognize faces. She has dinner again with Amal's family, this time bringing dessert and playing with the children without checking her phone.

She goes to museums and actually reads the plaques instead of taking the photos. She sits in parks and doesn't plan the next destination.

She practices being somewhere instead of between places.

It's uncomfortable and boring and occasionally wonderful.

---

On her last night in Berlin, Lin takes the S-Bahn to the airport observation deck. She watches planes take off and land, these massive machines of transit, carrying people to and from lives she'll never know.

She thinks about Petra, probably in Nairobi now, holding her grandson, learning how to be present across distance and differences.

She thinks about Amal, engineering water systems again, rebuilding professional identity planted in roots.

She thinks about her father, carrying the weight of what he left behind.

She thinks about her mother, waiting in Toronto with the patient stubbornness of someone who knows how to stay.

Lin pulls out her phone and books her flight to Seoul.

One way, like always.

But this time, it feels less like running and more like arriving.

---

On the train to the airport, Lin sits across from a young woman who looks lost—backpack, confused expression, phone out checking maps.

"First time in Berlin?" Lin asks.

"Yeah. Just arrived. I'm traveling for a year. Maybe more. Figuring things out."

Lin her twenty-four-year-old self in this woman. The certainty that movement equals meaning. The belief that you can outrun yourself.

"That's great," Lin says. And means it.

"Any advice?"

Lin thinks about all advice would have rejected six years ago, all the things she had to learn by exhausting herself against them.

"Yeah," she says finally. "The train eventually has to stop. And that's okay. Stopping isn't failing."

The woman smiles politely, clearly unconvinced.

Lin doesn't push it. Some things you can only learn in motion.

---

At the airport, Lin checks in for her flight to Seoul via Taipei.

The layover in Taipei is eight hours—long enough to leave the airport, though she hadn't originally planned to.

But sitting in the boarding area, she makes a decision.

She texts her grandmother—her father's mother, who she hasn't seen in four years: "I'm in Taipei for 8 hours tomorrow. Can I visit?"

The response comes quickly: "Of course. I'll make your lunch."

---

The flight boards. Lin finds her seat—middle row, middle seat, the least transient position possible.

She opens her laptop and writes an email to her students: "I'll be starting an in-person teaching position in Seoul soon. For those who want to continue online lessons, I'll have limited availability. For those ending our work together, thank you. You've taught me as much as I've taught you."

She writes one more email, to her mother: "After Seoul, maybe I'll visit Toronto. Stay for more than a weekend. Plant some actual roots. If you'll have me."

She sends both before she can second-guess.

---

The plane takes off, Berlin falling away below.

Lin opens a novel instead of her laptop. Lets herself exist in the hours between destinations without planning beyond them.

She thinks about roots and routes. About how maybe they're not opposites. About how you can be from multiple places, can carry multiple homes, as long as you're willing to actually be in them, not just pass through.

The plants—Margaret and Petra—are carefully packed in her carry-on. Absurd and impractical and exactly the kind of stupid hope she's been training herself not to have.

But she has them anyway.

And when the flight attendant brings her water, she asks for extra to keep them alive.

Because that's what you do when you're trying to grow something.

You give it water.

You give it light.

You stay long enough to see what blooms.
