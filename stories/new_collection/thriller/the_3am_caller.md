# The 3 AM Caller

**Day 1**

The phone rings at exactly 3:00 AM.

I know it's 3:00 AM because I'm staring at the clock when it happens. Have been for the past seven minutes, watching the digits shift from 2:59 to 3:00, waiting.

It always rings at 3:00 AM.

"Nightline Crisis Support, this is Maya speaking. You're safe here."

The line crackles. Background noise—traffic, maybe. Wind. It's hard to tell.

Then: breathing.

"Hello?" I try again. "I'm here to listen. Take your time."

More breathing. Rhythmic. Controlled. Not panicked. That's my first clue.

"I'm going to kill someone," the voice says.

Male. Mid-thirties, I'd guess. London accent. South, not East. Educated.

My training kicks in. Keep calm. Don't judge. Create connection.

"That sounds like you're in a lot of pain right now. Do you want to tell me what's happening?"

"You're not listening. I'm going to kill someone. Tomorrow night. 11:47 PM exactly."

"Okay. Let's talk about that. Why 11:47?"

"Because that's when the District Line train arrives at Richmond. Platform 2. She always takes that train."

"Who's she?"

Silence.

Then: "You'll see."

The line goes dead.

I sit there, headset still on, staring at the call log. Duration: 3 minutes, 17 seconds.

My supervisor, Ben, appears at my cubicle.

"Everything okay? You look pale."

"I—yeah. Just... weird caller."

"Threatening?"

"Maybe. I'm not sure."

Ben knows me well enough not to push. "Log it properly. Include timestamps. You need a break?"

"No. I'm fine."

But I'm not fine. Because here's the thing: this Is the fourth call I've received like this. Same voice. Same time. Same specificity.

And tomorrow is Wednesday.

**Wednesday - 11:47 PM**

I'm not supposed to be here.

Richmond Station is a 40-minute tube ride from my flat in King's Cross. I'm supposed to be asleep. I have a shift starting at midnight. Instead, I'm standing on platform 1, pretending to wait for a train, watching platform 2.

11:42: A handful of people scattered along the platform. An older man reading a book. Two university students, laughing about something on a phone. A woman in a navy coat talking into her mobile.

11:45: The train approaches. Yellow lights in the tunnel.

11:47: It arrives.

I watch everyone who boards. Everyone who disembarks. Twenty, maybe thirty people. No one seems in distress. No one screams. No police sirens.

Nothing happens.

I wait until 12:15. Take the last train back to King's Cross. Arrive at work ten minutes late.

"You look like hell," Ben says.

"Didn't sleep well."

"You're not usually late."

"I know. I'm sorry."

He studies me for a moment. "Maya, is everything alright? You've been off for a week now."

"I'm fine."

But I'm not fine. I'm exhausted. Paranoid. Jumping at shadows.

And at 3:00 AM, the phone rings again.

---

"Nightline Crisis Support, this is Maya speaking."

"You were there."

My blood goes cold. "I'm sorry?"

"Richmond Station. Platform 1. 11:42. You wore a gray coat."

Oh God.

"Who is this?"

"You're not listening. I told you. Wednesday night. 11:47 PM. Richmond. Platform 2."

"Nothing happened. I was there. I watched."

"Next Wednesday. Not this Wednesday. Next."

My hands are shaking. "Why are you doing this?"

"Because someone has to stop her."

Stop who?"

"The woman in the navy coat."

I remember her. Mid-forties, professional-looking. Talking on her phone.

"What did she do?"

"You'll understand when it happens."

"If you're planning to hurt someone, I need to—"

"You can't stop me. But you can bear witness. That's why I call you. Someone needs to know."

"Know what?"

"The truth."

The line goes dead.

---

**Thursday**

I don't tell Ben about the call.

I should. Protocol says I should. But if I do, they'll escalate it. Police involvement. Investigations. And based on what? A vague threat from an anonymous caller who might be delusional? Who might be testing me?

Crisis line callers say disturbing things all the time. Fantasies. Intrusive thoughts. Cries for attention.

But this feels different.

At lunch, I Google "Richmond Station incidents." Nothing recent. I search news from last Wednesday. Nothing.

I'm being paranoid. I'm falling for a manipulative caller's mind games.

Except—he knew I was there. He saw me.

That afternoon, I pull the recordings from the past four calls. Our system automatically archives everything. I listen to them back-to-back in the small listening room, headphones on, notepad ready.

**Call 1 (Three weeks ago)**

"I'm going to kill someone. Two weeks from tonight. 11:47 PM. Richmond Station. Platform 2."

**Call 2 (Two weeks ago)**

"One week left. Richmond. Platform 2. The woman in the navy coat."

**Call 3 (One week ago)**

"Wednesday. Tomorrow. Don't be late."

**Call 4 (Last night)**

"You were there. But you were watching the wrong Wednesday."

He's been escalating. Building to something. And I've been his audience.

I check the timestamp on Call 1: February 14th, 3:00:17 AM.

Count forward two weeks from that night.

The date: February 28th.

Yesterday was February 27th.

Next Wednesday is March 6th.

I feel sick.

---

**Friday**

I call in sick to work. First time in two years.

Ben texts: *U ok? Not like u.*

I don't respond.

Instead, I spend the day at the British Library, scrolling through newspaper archives. I don't know what I'm looking for exactly. A pattern. A previous incident. Something to make this make sense.

At 2:00 PM, I find it.

**"Richmond Station Death Under Investigation"**

*March 7th, 2012*

*Police are investigating the death of a woman who fell in front of a District Line train at Richmond Station Wednesday night. The incident occurred at approximately 11:45 PM. The woman, identified as Claire Hartley, 42, of Twickenham, was pronounced dead at the scene. The death is being treated as unexplained. Witnesses reported Ms. Hartley was alone on the platform when she fell. The driver reported no signs of distress or suicide ideation.*

March 7th, 2012.

A Wednesday.

Claire Hartley.

I search for more information. Find her obituary. A photo. Navy coat. Professional. Works in finance. No children. Survived by her husband, David Hartley.

I search for David Hartley.

And I find him.

---

**Saturday**

David Hartley is easy to find. He teaches philosophy at King's College London. Has an office in the Strand Building. Office hours: Tuesdays and Thursdays, 2-4 PM.

It's Saturday. The campus is quiet. The building is locked.

I stand outside, staring up at the windows, feeling foolish. What am I doing? Following a hunch based on a phone call from a potential stalker?

I should go home. I should tell Ben. I should hand this to the police and let them handle it.

Instead, I walk to Richmond.

---

The station looks different in daylight. Busier. More innocent.

I buy a coffee from the Pret on the high street and sit on a bench outside the station, watching people come and go. Families. Tourists. Commuters heading to late shifts.

At 5:00 PM, the light starts to fade.

At 6:00 PM, I'm still there.

At 7:00 PM, a man sits down next to me.

Mid-thirties. London accent. South, not East. Educated.

"You're Maya," he says.

---

I should run. I should scream. I should do anything except what I do, which is sit there, frozen.

"You look like your voice," he says. "Younger than I thought. How old are you? Twenty-six?"

"Twenty-eight."

"Close. I'm good with voices. You develop an ear for it, doing what I do."

"What do you do?"

He smiles. It doesn't reach his eyes.

"I teach philosophy. Specialization in ethics. Bit ironic, given the circumstances."

David Hartley.

"You're him," I whisper.

"And you're the woman who's been listening. I appreciate that. Most people don't listen properly anymore."

I find my voice. "Why are you calling me?"

"Because guilt needs a witness. And you seemed kind."

"Your wife—"

"Claire. Yes. She died here. Ten years ago last Wednesday. And it was my fault."

---

He tells me the story.

Not all at once. In pieces. Sitting on a bench outside Richmond Station while the evening darkens around us.

"Claire was having an affair," he begins. "Had been for months. I found out on Valentine's Day, 2012. Found the texts on her phone while she was in the shower.  A colleague. Some middle manager named Patrick."

He stares at the station entrance.

"I didn't confront her right away. I stewed in it. Let it eat me. By March, I was... not well. Angry. Obsessive. I started following her. Tracking her movements. I told myself I needed proof, but really, I just wanted to hurt her the way she'd hurt me."

A train rumbles past. He doesn't seem to notice.

"That Wednesday—March 6th, 2012—I followed her here. She was supposed to be working late, but she was meeting him. I saw them together on the platform. Kissing. Right there, out in the open."

He gestures toward platform 2.

"I confronted them. Caused a scene. Patrick left. Claire stayed. We argued. Said horrible things. And then her train came. She stepped toward it. I grabbed her arm. Pulled her back. Not to save her—to keep arguing. And she—"

He stops.

"She pulled away. Yanked her arm free. And she wasn't watching. She stepped back. Too far. Too fast. And she fell."

---

I don't breathe.

"The police ruled it accidental. The CCTV shows me grabbing her. Shows her pulling away. Shows her fall. But it doesn't show my face clearly. I was wearing a hood. And by the time they connected me to her—I'm her husband, I was always going to be a person of interest—I'd already lawyered up. Claimed I wasn't there. That I was home. And Patrick—Patrick corroborated my alibi, ironically. Thought I'd kill him if he didn't."

"And they believed you."

"They had no choice. Insufficient evidence. No witnesses came forward. I walked."

The bench creaks as he shifts position.

"But I didn't walk, did I? I've been here every Wednesday at 11:47 for ten years. Watching. Waiting. Punishing myself."

"The calls—"

"Started three weeks ago. After thirty years of therapy and medication and suppression, I decided I wanted to confess. But not to the police. They'd had their shot. I wanted to confess to someone who would... listen. Really listen."

"So you called a crisis line."

"At 3:00 AM. The darkest hour. When the filters come down."

---

We sit in silence for a long time.

"Are you recording this?" he asks finally.

"No."

"You should be. This is a confession."

"I'm not the police."

"I know. That's why I'm telling you."

He stands. Brushes off his coat.

"Next Wednesday, I'm going to be here again. 11:47 PM. I've done it every week for a decade. But this time, I think I'm going to step off the platform. Join Claire. Finish what should have happened ten years ago.

My throat tightens. "You can't—"

"I'm not asking for permission. I'm telling you. Because you deserve to know how it ends."

He walks away.

I should follow him. I should call someone. I should do something.

But I just sit there, watching him disappear into the station, and I think: I'm in over my head.

---

**Sunday**

I call the police.

Not about David Hartley. About the crisis calls.

I frame it as a wellbeing check: anonymous caller making vague threats, possibly suicidal, may need intervention. I give them the recording from the first call. Leave my details.

An officer called PC Anderson takes the report. Says they'll look into it.

I don't hear back.

---

**Monday**

Ben knows something's wrong.

"You going to tell me what's going on?" He catches me in the break room, making my fourth coffee of the shift.

"It's personal."

"Maya—"

"I'm handling it."

"Are you?" He studies my face. "Because you look like you haven't slept in days. And you've taken more breaks this week than in the last six months combined."

I want to tell him. Want to unload everything. But where would I even start?

"I'm fine," I lie.

He doesn't believe me. But he nods anyway.

"Okay. But if you need help—official or otherwise—you know where I am."

---

**Tuesday**

At 3:00 PM, I'm standing outside the Strand Building again.

David Hartley's office hours: 2-4 PM. I checked.

I take the elevator to the fourth floor. Find his office. The door is ajar.

I knock.

"Come in."

He's sitting behind a desk covered in papers and books. Half-moon reading glasses. Cardigan. He looks like exactly what he is: a middle-aged philosophy professor.

"Maya." He doesn't sound surprised. "I wondered if you'd come."

"I need you to not do this."

"Do what?"

"Wednesday. Richmond. You know what."

He removes his glasses. Rubs his eyes.

"And if I promised I wouldn't, would you believe me?"

"I don't know."

"Exactly. So what's the point of promising?"

"Because—" I'm fumbling. "Because I'm asking you to."

He smiles sadly. "You're kind. I was right about that."

"This isn't kindness. This is—I don't know what this is. But you can't ask me to carry this. To know what you're going to do and not try to stop you."

"I'm not asking you to carry anything. You can walk away. Forget this conversation. Forget the calls. Go back to your normal life."

"I can't."

"Why not?"

"Because you made me part of this! You turned me into a witness!"

"Yes." He nods slowly. "I did. I'm sorry for that."

He stands. Walks to the window. Looks out at the grey London sky.

"Can I tell you something? Claire was pregnant when she died."

I wasn't expecting that.

"She didn't know yet. But the autopsy revealed it. Six weeks. My child, presumably, though I'll never be certain."

His voice cracks.

"So I didn't just kill my wife. I killed my unborn child. And I've lived with that for ten years. And I'm tired, Maya. I'm so desperately tired."

---

**Wednesday - 11:30 PM**

I'm at Richmond Station again.

But this time, I'm not alone.

Ben insisted on coming when I finally told him everything. Not the official story. The real one. David Hartley. The confession. The plan.

"This is insane," Ben says. "We should call the police."

"I did. They didn't care."

"Then we call them again. Emphasize the suicide risk."

"And if that scares him off? If he goes somewhere else, somewhere I can't find him?"

Ben exhales sharply. "So your plan is what? Talk him down?"

"If I can."

"And if you can't?"

I don't have an answer to that.

---

11:45 PM.

Platform 2 is nearly empty. A young couple at the far end. An old woman with shopping bags.

No David Hartley.

11:46.

The train is coming. I can hear it in the tunnel.

11:47.

It arrives, slowing, brakes squealing.

And then I see him.

He's not on platform 2.

He's on platform 1. Directly across from where Ben and I are standing.

We lock eyes across the gap.

He smiles.

And steps off the platform.

---

**One Week Later**

The inquest rules it suicide.

David Hartley, 44, lecturer at King's College London, stepped in front of a District Line train at Richmond Station on March 14th, 2024, at approximately 11:48 PM. No suspicious circumstances. History of depression and suicidal ideation.

I give a statement to the police, explaining that I'd been in contact with Mr. Hartley regarding his mental health, that I'd attempted intervention, that I'd failed.

They don't ask too many questions.

---

At his memorial service, I sit in the back. There are maybe thirty people there. Colleagues, mostly. A sister. Some students.

The eulogy is standard. "Brilliant mind. Dedicated teacher. Tragic loss."

No mention of Claire. No mention of what happened ten years ago.

When it's over, an older woman approaches me.

"You're Maya, aren't you?"

I nod.

"I'm David's sister, Patricia. He mentioned you. Said you tried to help him."

"I tried. I'm sorry I couldn't—"

"Don't," she interrupts gently. "You gave him more than most people. You listened."

She hands me an envelope.

"He left this. Asked me to give it to you if... well."

---

I don't open the envelope until I'm home.

Inside: a single sheet of paper.

*Dear Maya,*

*If you're reading this, I've done what I promised. I'm sorry to have used you this way—as confessor, as witness, as the one person who might understand.*

*I lied to you about one thing. The train wasn't for Claire. Or the baby. Or guilt.*

*It was about choice.*

*For ten years, I've been a passenger. Carried along by circumstance. By cowardice. By the inability to make a decision one way or another.*

*Calling you was the first choice I'd made in a decade. And stepping off that platform was the second.*

*I chose to end it. Not because I was in pain—though I was. Not because I wanted forgiveness—I didn't deserve it. But because I wanted, for once, to be the author of my own story. To decide the ending.*

*Thank you for bearing witness. You made it mean something.*

*-D*

---

**Three Months Later - 3:00 AM**

The phone rings.

"Nightline Crisis Support, this is Maya speaking. You're safe here."

Breathing on the line. Traffic in the background.

My stomach drops.

"Hello?" I try to keep my voice steady. "I'm here to listen."

A long pause.

Then: "I don't know if I can do this anymore."

It's a woman. Young. Scottish accent. Crying.

Not David.

Never David again.

I exhale.

"That's okay. That's why I'm here. Let's talk about it. What's going on?"

And I listen.

Because that's what I do.

That's all I can do.

Even when it doesn't save them.

Even when it costs me everything.

I listen.

---

**Epilogue**

The station at Richmond has moved on. The 11:47 train still arrives every Wednesday. Platform 2 still sees hundreds of passengers each week.

No one remembers David Hartley.

No one except me.

And sometimes, when I can't sleep, I go back there. Stand on platform 1. Watch the trains come and go.

Looking for ghosts I'll never find.

Listening for calls that will never come.

Waiting.

Always waiting.

For the next 3:00 AM.
